## Claims reviewed
1. 85% of teachers and 86% of students reported using AI during the 2024-2025 school year, according to a Center for Democracy and Technology report.
2. Large K-12 school districts including New York City, Seattle, and Los Angeles initially implemented outright bans on generative AI tools like ChatGPT.
3. At least 31 states—recently joined by Montana, Maine, Alaska, Nevada, and Michigan—have adopted official AI education policy guidelines.
4. Ohio and Tennessee have enacted laws requiring individual school districts to develop their own comprehensive AI policies.
5. Columbia University and the University of Utah require professors to clearly outline their AI rules in course syllabi.
6. Turnitin, GPTZero, and Copyleaks are widely used AI plagiarism detection tools.
7. Studies have demonstrated that these detection tools are unreliable, suffering from high false positive rates and biases against non-native English speakers and neurodiverse students.
8. The University of Texas at Austin has prohibited the unauthorized use of third-party AI detection software altogether, citing concerns over student intellectual property rights and potential violations of FERPA.

## Recommended edits for editor
*   **Soften** the claim about Columbia University and the University of Utah requiring AI rules in syllabi. Change "require" to "strongly encourage" or "advise," as there is no strict university-wide mandate.
*   **Add attribution** to the Center for Democracy and Technology for the 85% and 86% statistics.
*   **Add attribution** to Education Week for the claim that Ohio and Tennessee are the only two states requiring district AI policies.
